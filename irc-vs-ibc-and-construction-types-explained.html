IRC vs. IBC and Construction Types, Explained

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Line That Divides Housing Into Two Code Worlds

Every residential project in the United States lands on one side of a single dividing line, and which side determines the rules it must follow for the rest of its life. On one side is the International Residential Code, the streamlined rulebook for houses. On the other is the International Building Code, the commercial code that governs apartment buildings. The line runs through the middle of the housing spectrum, and multi-family projects sit squarely on the commercial side - a fact that reshapes cost, design, and complexity far more than most people outside the industry realize.

What the IRC Covers

The International Residential Code is a self-contained, prescriptive code for detached houses, duplexes, and townhouses up to three stories tall. Its virtue is simplicity: it provides recipe-like requirements a builder can follow without extensive engineering, which is why single-family construction is comparatively quick to permit and build. Townhouses - single-family units side by side, each with its own entrance and separated by fire walls from ground to roof - are the IRC's upper boundary. Push past that boundary, and the residential code no longer applies.

Where the IBC Takes Over

The International Building Code governs everything the IRC does not: apartment buildings, larger condominium structures, mixed-use buildings, and any residential structure beyond the townhouse threshold. The IBC classifies buildings by occupancy - multi-family residential falls into a residential occupancy group - and by construction type, then applies an interlocking system of requirements for fire resistance, egress, sprinklers, and accessibility. Where the IRC hands a builder a recipe, the IBC demands engineered design and rigorous plan review. The same building trade, the same lumber, but a fundamentally more complex regulatory framework.

Construction Types, Explained

The IBC's central organizing idea for the building itself is the construction type - a classification based on how fire-resistant the structure's materials are. The types run along a spectrum:

Broad category Materials Height & area allowed
Noncombustible (fire-rated) Protected steel, concrete Greatest - high-rise capable
Noncombustible (limited) Steel, concrete, less protection Large mid-rise
Combustible (protected wood) Fire-rated wood-frame assemblies Moderate - common for mid-rise
Combustible (standard wood) Conventional wood-frame Lowest - small low-rise

The rule the table encodes is simple: the more fire-resistant the construction, the taller and larger the building may be. A concrete-and-steel building can rise many stories; a conventional wood-frame building is held to a few. This trade-off between construction cost and allowable size is one of the central economic decisions in multi-family design.

The Wood-Frame Sweet Spot

Most low- and mid-rise apartment construction uses wood framing, because it is dramatically cheaper than steel or concrete while still reaching several stories under the code's rules. A common and economical configuration places multiple stories of wood-frame apartments over a noncombustible concrete ground floor or podium - the podium satisfies the code for the ground level's uses and lets the wood floors above reach a height that pure wood-frame could not. This "podium" approach dominates mid-rise multi-family precisely because it captures most of wood's cost advantage while stretching the height the code allows.

Why the IRC/IBC Distinction Drives Everything

The jump from IRC to IBC is where multi-family cost and complexity originate. IBC construction types cap the building's size, which sets the yield and the economics. IBC fire, egress, and accessibility requirements shape the floor plan and add systems the IRC never demands. And IBC plan review is a rigorous engineered process, not a checklist. A developer who understands that a project is an IBC building - and which construction type it will use - can price and design it realistically from the start. One who assumes house-building rules apply is in for expensive surprises, which is why identifying the governing code and construction type is among the first moves in any building-systems analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

When does a project switch from the IRC to the IBC?

When it exceeds the IRC's scope - generally beyond one- and two-family dwellings and townhouses up to three stories. Apartment buildings and larger multi-family structures are IBC (commercial code) projects, with all the additional requirements that brings.

Are townhouses IRC or IBC?

Townhouses up to three stories, each unit separated by fire walls and with its own entrance, generally fall under the IRC. This makes them simpler to build than apartment buildings, which is one reason townhouse-style multi-family is a popular gentle-density form.

What is a construction type?

An IBC classification based on the fire resistance of a building's structural materials, from noncombustible steel and concrete to combustible wood frame. The type interacts with allowed height and area - more fire-resistant construction permits larger buildings.

Why is wood-frame construction so common for apartments?

Cost. Wood framing is far cheaper than steel or concrete and still reaches several stories under the code, especially in a podium configuration with a noncombustible ground floor. It is the economical sweet spot for low- and mid-rise multi-family.

What is podium construction?

A configuration placing stories of wood-frame apartments over a noncombustible concrete base or "podium," often used for parking or retail. It combines wood's cost advantage above with the code compliance and height benefit of a noncombustible ground level.

Does construction type affect how tall I can build?

Directly. Height and area allowances rise with the fire resistance of the construction type - noncombustible steel and concrete permit high-rises, while conventional wood frame is limited to a few stories. The choice trades construction cost against allowable size.

Why does the code distinction matter to project cost?

Because IBC buildings carry construction-type limits, engineered fire and egress systems, sprinklers, and accessibility requirements the IRC never imposes. Knowing a project is an IBC building - and its construction type - is what lets a team price and design it accurately from the outset.

Who decides which code and construction type applies?

The governing code is set by the project's size and occupancy under the jurisdiction's adopted code, not by preference - a building's scope places it under the IRC or IBC automatically. Within the IBC, the design team selects a construction type that satisfies the desired height and area, then designs the structure to meet that type's fire-resistance requirements. The local building official reviews and confirms the classification at permitting, so the choice is a design decision made against fixed rules rather than a negotiation.

Can a single project mix construction types?

Yes - the podium configuration is exactly this, pairing a noncombustible base with wood-frame floors above, and the code has provisions for treating such buildings as separated or combined for height-and-area purposes. Mixing types is a deliberate strategy to capture the height benefit of noncombustible construction at the ground level while keeping the cost advantage of wood above, and it is one of the most common ways mid-rise multi-family reaches its economical sweet spot.
