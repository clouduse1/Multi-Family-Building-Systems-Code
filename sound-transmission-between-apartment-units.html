Sound Transmission Between Apartment Units

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Complaint That Empties Apartment Buildings

Ask a property manager why tenants do not renew, and near the top of the list - above rent, above finishes - sits noise. Footsteps from above, conversations through a wall, a neighbor's television at midnight: sound transmission between units is the quality-of-life issue residents feel most acutely and forgive least. The building code recognizes this by setting minimum sound-isolation standards between dwellings, but the minimum is exactly that - a floor, not a target. Understanding how sound moves between apartments, and how the assemblies between them are rated, separates buildings people stay in from buildings they escape.

Two Kinds of Sound, Two Ratings

Sound travels between units in two fundamentally different ways, and each has its own rating. Airborne sound - voices, music, television - travels through the air and vibrates the wall or floor that then re-radiates it into the neighbor's space. Its resistance is measured by the Sound Transmission Class, or STC: higher numbers block more airborne sound. Impact sound - footsteps, dropped objects, furniture dragged across a floor - is generated by physical contact with the structure and transmitted directly through it, felt most through floor-ceiling assemblies. Its resistance is measured by the Impact Insulation Class, or IIC, again with higher numbers performing better. A wall or floor must handle both, and the two require different design responses.

Rating Blocks Typical code minimum
STC (Sound Transmission Class) Airborne sound - voices, music, TV 50 between dwelling units
IIC (Impact Insulation Class) Impact sound - footsteps, drops 50 for floor-ceiling assemblies

The building code commonly sets a minimum of 50 for both STC and IIC between dwelling units - a level that prevents the worst intrusions but still lets a fair amount through. Field-measured performance typically runs several points below the laboratory rating, so a design that just meets 50 on paper can disappoint in the finished building, which is why quality-focused builders design above the minimum.

How Assemblies Block Sound

Three physical principles quiet an assembly, and good design uses all of them. Mass resists sound because heavier constructions vibrate less - concrete floors and double layers of drywall block more than light single layers. Decoupling breaks the path sound takes through the structure: resilient channels, sound clips, and separated framing prevent one side of a wall from directly driving the other. And absorption inside the cavity - insulation batts - soaks up sound energy before it crosses. A high-performing separation wall or floor typically combines all three: substantial mass, a decoupled structure, and an absorptive cavity. Impact sound on floors adds a fourth tool - a resilient surface layer or underlayment that cushions the footstep before it reaches the structure.

The Flanking Problem

The most frustrating sound failures are not through the rated wall at all - they travel around it. Flanking is sound that bypasses the separation assembly through connected paths: a continuous floor slab, a shared framing member, ductwork, back-to-back electrical outlets, or a gap where the wall meets the floor. A beautifully rated wall can be undone by a flanking path that lets sound sneak past it, which is why sound control, like fire separation, depends on continuity and careful detailing. Sealing gaps, offsetting outlets, isolating penetrations, and breaking continuous structural paths are what turn a good rating on paper into quiet in practice.

Why Sound Is a Business Decision, Not Just a Code Item

Fire ratings are pass-fail at inspection; sound performance is felt every day by the people paying rent. A building that meets the acoustic minimum and no more invites the noise complaints that drive turnover, while one that invests in above-code separation earns the retention and reputation that make it more valuable. Because the cost of better sound isolation is modest when designed in from the start - and enormous to fix after construction - the acoustic assemblies are a decision experienced developers make deliberately rather than defaulting to the minimum. Getting sound right, integrated with the fire-rated and structural assemblies it shares walls with, is part of what a complete building-systems-and-code analysis addresses.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between STC and IIC?

STC (Sound Transmission Class) measures resistance to airborne sound like voices and music; IIC (Impact Insulation Class) measures resistance to impact sound like footsteps. Walls are rated by STC, floor-ceiling assemblies by both, and higher numbers block more sound in each case.

What sound rating does code require between apartments?

Codes commonly require a minimum of 50 for both STC and IIC between dwelling units. This prevents the worst noise intrusion but still allows a fair amount through, which is why quality-focused builders design above the minimum.

Why do apartments still sound loud if they meet code?

Because the code minimum is a floor, not a comfort target, and field performance usually measures several points below the lab rating. A wall designed to just meet the minimum can disappoint in the finished building, especially against footsteps and bass.

How do you make a wall or floor quieter?

By combining mass (heavier construction), decoupling (resilient channels or separated framing that breaks the sound path), and absorption (insulation in the cavity). Floors add a resilient underlayment to cushion impact. High-performing assemblies use all of these together.

What is flanking sound?

Sound that bypasses a rated wall or floor through connected paths - a continuous slab, shared framing, ducts, or back-to-back outlets - rather than passing through the assembly itself. Even a well-rated wall can be undone by flanking, which is why continuity and detailing matter.

Is impact or airborne sound harder to control?

Impact sound is often the more stubborn, because footsteps put energy directly into the structure. It typically requires resilient floor underlayments and decoupled ceiling assemblies, beyond the mass and absorption that help with airborne sound.

Why invest above the sound-code minimum?

Because noise is a leading driver of tenant turnover, and better acoustic isolation is inexpensive to design in but costly to retrofit. Above-code sound performance improves retention and reputation, making it a deliberate business decision rather than a default to the minimum.
