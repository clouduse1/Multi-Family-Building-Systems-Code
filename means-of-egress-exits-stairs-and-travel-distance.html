Means of Egress: Exits, Stairs, and Travel Distance

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Code's Promise: Everyone Gets Out

Of all the systems in a multi-family building, none is more heavily regulated than the way people leave it in an emergency. The building code calls this the means of egress - the continuous, protected path from any point in the building to the safety of the outside - and it governs that path with a precision that surprises anyone seeing it for the first time. Exit counts, stair widths, travel distances, door swings, corridor ratings, lighting, signage: all are prescribed, because egress is the last line of defense when fire suppression and separation have done all they can. Understanding egress explains a great deal about why apartment floor plans look the way they do.

The Three Parts of the Path

Code divides the means of egress into three connected segments, each with its own rules. The exit access is the path from within a unit or space to an exit - the travel across a room and down a corridor. The exit itself is the protected element that provides a safe route through the building, most often an enclosed, fire-rated stairwell. The exit discharge is the final segment from the bottom of the exit to the public way outside. The code's goal is a path that is continuous and progressively more protected as occupants move along it - from the unprotected room, into the rated corridor, into the fire-separated stair, and out.

How Many Exits, and How Far

Two rules shape the floor plan more than any others. The first is the number of exits: most floors of a multi-family building must have at least two separate, remote exits, so that a fire blocking one still leaves another available. The remoteness matters - two exits next to each other can both be cut off by a single fire, so code requires them separated by a minimum distance. The second rule is travel distance: the maximum allowed length from the most remote point on a floor to an exit, capped so no one is ever too far from escape. Sprinklered buildings are allowed longer travel distances, one of the many ways the fire-protection systems interlock.

Sizing the Path to the Crowd

Egress components are not one-size-fits-all; they are sized to the occupant load - the number of people the code assumes could be present. Wider corridors and stairs are required as the population served grows, because the path must physically carry everyone moving at once without dangerous crowding. This is why a large building's stairwells are generously proportioned and why corridor widths are not left to preference. The math connects the building's size to the physical dimensions of its escape routes.

The Protected Stairwell

In a multi-story building the stairwell is the heart of egress, and code protects it accordingly. It is enclosed in fire-rated construction, entered through self-closing rated doors, kept clear of storage and obstruction, and often pressurized or provided with smoke control so it stays breathable while people descend. Emergency lighting keeps it usable in a power failure, and illuminated signage marks the way. The stairwell is engineered to remain a survivable island while the rest of the floor may be compromised - the single most important space in the building during an emergency.

Why Egress Shapes the Whole Plan

Because egress rules are non-negotiable and geometric, they dictate the skeleton of a multi-family floor plan before the units are even arranged. Two remote stairwells must be placed and separated; corridors must connect every unit to them within the travel-distance limit; widths must match the occupant load. Designers lay out the egress system first and fit the apartments around it, because a plan that violates an egress rule is not buildable at any price. This is why experienced multi-family design begins with the exits - and why egress sits alongside fire protection and construction type as a pillar of any building-systems-and-code analysis.

What does "means of egress" mean?

The code term for the complete, protected path out of a building in an emergency - from any point inside, through corridors and a protected stair, to the public way outside. It is divided into exit access, the exit itself, and the exit discharge, each progressively more protected.

How many exits does a multi-family floor need?

Most floors require at least two separate exits, positioned a minimum distance apart so a single fire cannot block both. The remoteness requirement is as important as the count - two exits close together do not provide real redundancy.

What is travel distance?

The maximum allowed length from the most remote point on a floor to the nearest exit, capped by code so no occupant is ever too far from escape. Sprinklered buildings are permitted longer travel distances because suppression buys additional safety margin.

Why are stairwells enclosed and fire-rated?

So the exit stays usable during the fire the rest of the floor may be experiencing. A protected stairwell - rated enclosure, self-closing doors, often smoke control and emergency lighting - remains a survivable path while occupants descend to safety.

How are corridor and stair widths determined?

By the occupant load - the number of people the code assumes the space could hold. Wider paths are required as that population grows, so the egress system can physically carry everyone moving at once without dangerous crowding.

Why do exits have to be remote from each other?

Because two exits close together can both be cut off by a single fire, defeating the purpose of having two. Code requires a minimum separation so that if one exit is blocked, the other remains reachable from another direction.

Why does egress shape the floor plan so much?

Because its rules are geometric and non-negotiable - stair placement, separation, corridor connections, and travel distances must all be satisfied before units are arranged. Designers lay out egress first and fit apartments around it, since a plan that breaks an egress rule cannot be built.
